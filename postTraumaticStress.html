What Is Post Traumatic Stress Disorder?

Posttraumatic stress disorder (PTSD), once called shell shock or battle fatigue syndrome, is a serious condition that can develop after a person has experienced or witnessed a traumatic or terrifying event in which there was serious physical harm or threat.

Symptoms Of Post Traumatic Stress Disorder

Symptoms of PTSD often are grouped into four main categories, including:

  • Recurrent, unwanted distressing memories of the traumatic event
  • Reliving: People with PTSD repeatedly relive the ordeal through thoughts and memories of the trauma. These may include flashbacks, hallucinations, and nightmares. They also may feel great distress when certain things remind them of the trauma, such as the anniversary date of the event.
  • Thinking about a problem over and over again and unable to stop (rumination)Avoiding: The person may avoid people, places, thoughts, or situations that may remind them of the trauma. This can lead to feelings of detachment and isolation from family and friends, as well as a loss of interest in activities that the person once enjoyed.
  • Increased arousal: These include excessive emotions; problems relating to others, including feeling or showing affection; difficulty falling or staying asleep; irritability; outbursts of anger; difficulty concentrating; and being "jumpy" or easily startled. The person may also suffer physical symptoms, such as increased blood pressure and heart rate, rapid breathing, muscle tension, nausea, and diarrhea.
  • Negative cognitions and mood: This refers to thoughts and feelings related to blame, estrangement, and memories of the traumatic event.
  • Reliving the traumatic event as if it were happening again (flashbacks)
  • Upsetting dreams or nightmares about the traumatic event
  • Changes in physical and emotional reactions
  • Avoiding places, activities or people that remind you of the traumatic event
  • Feeling detached from family and friends
  • Feeling emotionally numb

Causes of Post Traumatic Stress Disorder

Genetics

Post-traumatic stress disorder can be caused due to depression and anxiety disorders that run in families. Mental health issues can increase the body’s stress response, which will result in development of PTSD.

Brain chemistry

Brain regulates certain chemicals and hormones that are responsible for emotions, such as fear, anxiety, and depression. Any imbalances in the brain chemicals that regulate these typical emotions can cause post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD).

Psychological Stress

Certain types of notable mental illnesses, such as extreme stress, anger, depression, and anxiety can cause PTSD.

Traumatic Events

People who have experienced trauma during their earlier life, such as physical abuse during childhood.

Drug withdrawal or misuse

People facing problems with substance abuse, such as excessive alcohol consumption or drug addiction.

Treatment of Post Traumatic Stress Disorder

Treatments for Post Traumatic Stress Disorder include:

Medication:

Several types of drugs are used to treat post traumatic stress disorders. Talk to your doctor or psychiatrist about the pros and cons of each medicine to decide which one is best for you.

Psychotherapy:

This is a type of counseling that helps you learn how your emotions affect your behaviors. It’s sometimes called talk therapy. A trained mental health specialist listens and talks to you about your thoughts and feelings and suggests ways to understand and manage them and your post traumatic stress disorder.

Stay happy:

Encourage the patient to lead a stress-free life, by practicing yoga and meditation.

Community:

Get together with friends, keep a journal and get rid of your negative thoughts.
